# 03 -- Editorial Magazine

## Design Philosophy

Content-rich, magazine-style layout where reading IS the experience. Large hero images, pull quotes, multi-column text, and elegant typography create a page that feels like a well-produced publication. The design serves the words, not the other way around.

## UX Pattern

The user arrives at a cinematic hero that feels like a magazine cover -- full-width image with overlaid headline and byline. A thin scroll progress bar (accent colored) tracks reading position at the top of the viewport. The hero image has subtle parallax at a 0.3 speed ratio.

Below the hero, content flows in a rich-text section with large serif body type and a 2-column layout on desktop. Pull quotes slide in from the side on scroll. A full-width image break with a caption (which fades in after the image enters the viewport) provides visual breathing room.

The features section uses editorial cards with large images, category labels, and short excerpts -- like a magazine article grid. Testimonials are arranged in a masonry/staggered layout with photos. Stats use elegant type with thin horizontal rules between items. The FAQ section uses smooth accordions with thin borders and generous line height. The page closes with a newsletter-style CTA that feels like a magazine subscription, not a sales pitch.

A reading time estimate near the headline sets expectations upfront.

## When to Use

- Content-heavy businesses that publish case studies, thought pieces, or long-form writing
- Agencies that want to showcase their work through storytelling, not just screenshots
- Professional services firms (law, architecture, consulting) where credibility comes from depth
- Thought leadership platforms, author sites, or editorial brands
- Any project where the written content is the primary asset and needs room to breathe

## When NOT to Use

- Brands with very little written content or short attention-span audiences
- Highly interactive products (SaaS dashboards, tools) where the UI is the focus
- Playful or irreverent brands that need energy and motion over editorial calm
- Projects with aggressive conversion goals that need every section to push toward a CTA
- Mobile-first audiences where multi-column layouts lose their impact
